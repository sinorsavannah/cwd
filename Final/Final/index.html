Is nuclear power renewable?

That is the a big debate within environmental science and environmental politics. It comes down to semantics, what do you mean when you say renewable? Do you mean energy coming from a sources that is constantly replenished or do you mean a source that is sustainable for the environment (aka green energy)? Using the first definition, which is the technical definition, then nuclear does not fit that classification because nuclear power is derived from radioactive fuel of which there is a finite supply. But if you mean is it sustainable then it could potentially fit within the category.

Herein lies the debate. One side will point out the nuclear power does not emit greenhouse gases therefor it is not polluting the earth. The otherside would say that nuclear does produce nuclear waste. That radioactive waste stays radioactive for tens of thousands of years and is unsafe for humans to be around. So, nuclear power is more sustainable than fossil fuels but not as much as a true renewable power source

BTU stands for British Thermal Unit and it is way to measure heat or energy. One BTU is equivalent to approximately 1055.06 joules or 0.293071 watt hour, which are units you might be more familiar with. One BTU is also the amount of heat you need to warm a pound of water up by 1 degree fahrenheit, if it is easier to think of it that way. A quadrillion BTU, which is our unit of measurement, is 1015 BTU... so a lot.

Not only are we producing more renewable and nuclear now, we are also consuming more. Looking at the pie chart above, if you click play and allow it to progress through the time line, you'll see the pie slices representing renewable and nuclear power growing every year. These two charts show how the make up of U.S. total consumption and production has changed over time but let us now turn to how production and consumption have changed relative to eachother.

Primary imports and exports are great proxy indicator for overall consumption and production. We import when we consume more than we produce, and we export when we produce more than we consume. As such, you can see that both consumption and production have both been increasing. However, production has started to outpace consumption as of 2019.

Our exports started rising rapidly in the early 2000s, and it's important to dig into why. During that time, we discovered new fracking and other drilling practices, which allowed us to access previously inaccessible deposits of natural gas and oil throughout the U.S. This new technology resulted in the U.S. being the #1 exporter for natural oil and gas. While being an exporter instead of an importer does provide energy security, these resources are limited, and we cannot rely on them forever. For energy security and for the sake of the environment we need to consider diversifying our sources of energy.

Urban and Rural Utility-scale PV: PV stands for photovoltaic which refers to solar panels. These are large scale solar panel farms either in cities or rural areas.

Rooftop PV: These are small scale solar panels on the top of people's homes and businesses.

CSP: Stands for Concentrated Solar Power. It uses mirrors to focus light and generate high-temperature heat which produces electricity via steam turbines

Onshore Wind: A wind farm that is onland, typically these are in the midwest.

Offshore Wind: A wind farm that is either off the coast or in the great lakes. The northeast in the U.S. is a good place for offshore wind.

Biopower-solid/gaseous: Biopower comes from burning or converting biomass to heat or electricity. Biomass is organic material. Biopower can come from solid biomass such as woodchips or agricultural waste or gaseous biomass. Gaseous biomass comes from a process that uses bacteria to break down the solid waste (mainly animal manure and human sewage) and produce methane which can be used for internal combusition engines, turbines, and fuels cells to generate electricity.

Geothermal Hydrothermal and EGS Geothermal: Geothermal power harnesses the heat from Earth's interior for electricity generation. Hydrothermal is the traditional method which uses naturally occuring heat, water, and permeability to generate heat whereas EGS (enhanced geothermal systems) is new technology that can create artificial reservoirs in areas that lack natural permeability.

Hydropower: Uses the natural flow of water to to turn turbines.

Lets starts with GWh vs GW aka giga-watt hours vs giga-watts. So a watt is a unit of power and a giga-watt is one billion watts. What is the difference between a giga-watt and a giga-watt hour? Adding in that time element changes the unit from one of power to one of energy. Energy is a measurement how much power is generated over a given time, so in this instance how many giga-watts over the course of one hour. Giga-watt would be used for capactiy whereas giga-watt hours would be used for usage and generation measurements.

BDT is a bone dry ton. It is equivalent to 2000 pounds of organic materials with zero percent moisture. This would be used to measure biomass like wood.
