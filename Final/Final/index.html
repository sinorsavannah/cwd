Energy

Production, Consumption, and History in the U.S.

Overview

Since industrialization, our country has been producing mass amounts of energy to power everything from factories to household appliances. We use electricity for practically everything nowadays. It is hard to go a day without using it in some form or another. So where does it come from?

The answer may suprise you...

BTU stands for British Thermal Unit and it is way to measure heat or energy. One BTU is equivalent to approximately 1055.06 joules or 0.293071 watt hour, which are units you might be more familiar with. One BTU is also the amount of heat you need to warm a pound of water up by 1 degree fahrenheit, if it is easier to think of it that way. A quadrillion BTU, which is our unit of measurement, is 1015 BTU... so a lot.

As we can see from the graph above, our source of energy here in the U.S. has changed over time. Focusing on 1949 on wards we can see trends move from nearly entire production being fossil fuel based to now including renewable and nuclear energy.

But perhaps more suprising is that renewables existed all the way back in 1949. When you think of renewables do you think of high tech solar panels? Well those are a great example of modern renewable energy, we have been ale to harness power from the wind and water for centuries. Renewables aren't as new as you might think.

Not only are we producing more energy from renewables at the time, we are also consuming more. The pie slice that is renewable power consumption in the U.S. gets bigger every year. The nuclear power grows too. It took us a bit longer to figure out how to get nuclear power working, but once it is on, it's on.

As you can see, overall consumption and production are increasing, A good way to track that is imports and exports. We import when we consume more than we produce, and we export when we produce more than we consume. Over the timeframe we can see both have increased but production had outpaced consumption. Our exports rise rapidly over the 2000s, and it's important to dig into why. During that time, we discover new shaling and other drilling practices, which allow us to access previously inaccessible deposits of natural gas and oil throughout the U.S. This new tech resulted in us being the #1 exporter for natural oil and gas. While being an exporter instead of an importer does provide energy security, these resources are limited, and we cannot rely on them forever.

Uh no. So there might be some units here you haven't seen before or know the difference between.

Lets starts with GWh vs GW aka giga-watt hours vs giga-watts. So a watt is a unit of power and a giga-watt is one billion watts. What is the difference between a giga-watt and a giga-watt hour? Adding in that time element changes the unit from one of power to one of energy. Energy is a measurement how much power is generated over a given time, so in this instance how many giga-watts over the course of one hour. Giga-watt would be used for capactiy whereas giga-watt hours would be used for usage and generation measurements.

BDT is a bone dry ton. It is equivalent to 2000 pounds of organic materials with zero percent moisture. This would be used to measure biomass like wood.

If not fossil fuel, then what? We've already seen that we are using renewable and nuclear energy, but we aren't using them to their fullest potential. All of these things are things we are already doing in the U.S., but with public support and government funding, we could revolutionize the energy industry in America.
